Trading Basics — Zero To The Model

This tab is the plain-English floor under everything else. No jargon — any shorthand you meet elsewhere in the course is decoded on the Key tab.

1 · What are we actually doing?

We trade XAU/USD — the price of one ounce of gold, quoted in US dollars. We never own any gold. We bet on whether that number goes up or down, and we can profit in either direction:

BUY (go "long")you profit if the price RISES after you enter
SELL (go "short")you profit if the price FALLS after you enter

Selling something you don't own feels odd at first. The broker handles the mechanics — all you decide is direction, size, and exit points.

2 · How price moves, and the spread

Gold moving from 4,040 to 4,050 is a $10 move. All our stops and targets are measured in these dollars of gold price.

Your platform always shows two prices — e.g. SELL 4,038.5 / BUY 4,039.3. The gap (the spread) is the broker's cut, so every trade starts slightly negative. That's one reason we only take trades aiming for big moves (2R), never tiny scalps.

3 · The three orders every trade uses

1
EntryWe enter at the open of the next H1 candle after a valid signal. No chasing, no early entries.
2
Stop-lossA pre-set order that closes the trade automatically at our maximum acceptable loss. Placed at the opposite side of the box. It is NEVER moved further away.
3
Take-profitA pre-set order that banks the win automatically at the 2R target.
Set all three, then hands off. If neither exit is hit within 18 hours, the trade is closed manually. The marker draws all of this for you as red and green zones.

4 · R — the only unit that matters

Every trade risks the same fixed slice of the account: 0.25%. That amount is called 1R. Wins and losses are counted in R, not pounds, so every trade is comparable.

Worked example: account £10,000 → 1R = £25. Say the stop is $8 of gold price away from entry. You size the position so that an $8 move against you loses exactly £25. Hit the 2R target: +£50. Hit the stop: −£25.

10 tradesResult
4 winners at +2R+8R
6 losers at −1R−6R
Month total+2R (profitable)

Read that table again: we lost MORE trades than we won and still made money. That's the whole secret — winners are twice the size of losers, so a roughly 50% win rate is enough. Losing streaks are normal and survivable because each loss is only 0.25%.

5 · Why time of day is part of the system

All times UK:

23:00–06:00Asia trades quietly overnight — gold drifts in a range. This builds "the box".
08:00–19:00London then New York wake up with real volume. Only H1 closes in this window can be signals.
21:00 onwardsMarket rollover — spreads widen, moves are unreliable. No new trades.

Big scheduled news (US interest-rate decisions, the monthly US jobs report) makes price jump randomly through levels — we sit out a 4-hour window around those.

6 · What a prop firm is (the actual plan)

A proprietary-trading ("prop") firm rents you a large trading account. You pay a one-off challenge fee (~£100–£500), pass their evaluation — hit a modest profit target without breaking their loss rules — and then trade their capital, keeping ~80% of profits as payouts.

Their rules (daily loss limit, max drawdown) reward exactly one skill: discipline. That's why this course drills rules, not predictions. The path: learn → demo proof (60 days, journalled) → evaluation → funded. The challenge fee is the only money ever at risk.

non-negotiable

Golden rules for beginners

  • One trade per day, maximum. After a trade — win or lose — you're done.
  • The stop-loss never moves further away. Not once, not "just this time".
  • A wick is not a close. Only where the candle FINISHES counts (see Candles tab).
  • A clean skip is a win. Most days the correct trade is no trade.
  • News window = locked. No exceptions, however good it looks.
  • 0.25% risk, always. Never "a bit more because I'm confident".
  • When unsure, read the marker's Action row. If it says DON'T TRADE, don't trade.

read the chart

Candles — What We Look For

Every candle on our chart summarises one hour of trading. Learn to read one candle and you can read the whole chart.

Anatomy of a candle

The fat part is the body: where the hour opened and closed. The thin lines are wicks: price visited there but didn't stay. Green = finished higher, red = finished lower.

The close is the truth. Until the hour ends, a candle is still being written — a "breakout" at 25 minutes past can vanish by the close. We only ever act on FINISHED candles.

The signal we want: breakout CLOSE

signal The green candle's body finishes above box + buffer, between 08:00 and 19:00 UK, with both trend filters agreeing. Entry is the open of the NEXT candle. (Mirror image below the box low for a SELL.)

The trap we skip: wick-only break

no trade Price spiked over the line and got rejected — the body finished back inside the box. This is the market hunting impatient traders. A wick is never a signal, however dramatic it looks.

Clean box vs messy box

Top: small, orderly overnight candles — a calm range worth trading a break from. Bottom: one violent candle IS most of the box (spike > 0.65) — the range is one panic move, not a real balance. The marker grades this for you (Range / Updates / Spike rows), but your eye should agree.

Everything below this line is chart LANGUAGE, not chart RULES. If it isn't box + close + trend, it's a skip — whatever shape the candles make.
vocabulary, not signals

Chart language — words you'll hear online

YouTube and trading Twitter speak in these terms. Learn what they MEAN so you're never lost — and notice that the good ones are already inside our system with stricter definitions.

WordWhat it means — and our stance
ConsolidationPrice resting sideways in a balance of buyers and sellers — small overlapping candles. Our Asian box IS a consolidation — one specific, quality-graded one.
BreakoutPrice escaping a consolidation. Ours only counts with an H1 CLOSE beyond box + buffer in the right session with trend agreement.
ConfluenceSeveral independent reasons agreeing at once. Our checklist IS confluence — clean box + daily bias + EMA stack + session + no lockout — except ours is backtested, not vibes.
ConfirmationWaiting for proof before acting. For us the H1 close IS the confirmation. A wick, a poke, a feeling — not confirmation.
BOS"Break of structure" — a close beyond the last swing high/low, suggesting the trend continues. Kindred spirit to our breakout close. As an EXTRA filter it's a lab ticket under test — not a rule yet.
CHoCH"Change of character" — the first structure break AGAINST the trend; an early reversal warning. Journal-note vocabulary only.
Bull / bear flagA strong move (the pole), then a small sloping pause (the flag), often continuing. Nice to recognise; never an entry reason here.
Reversal patternsShapes read as a trend turning — double top/bottom, head & shoulders. Untested lore for us. Note it, never trade it.
Cup & handleA long rounded base plus a small dip, then a breakout (yes — the mug). Same verdict: vocabulary, not a signal.
The pattern words describe what price DID. Our edge is a tested rule for what WE do. If a pattern idea ever looks strong, it goes to the lab for a backtest ticket — it never walks straight onto the checklist.
the tools

Indicators — What Each One Does

Only four things sit on our chart, and each has exactly one job. Nothing here predicts — they measure.

━ ━ ━ EMA 20 / 50 / 200 — the H1 trend

A moving average smooths recent prices into one line ("average of the last N hours"). The E is for exponential — recent hours count more. Three of them make the trend readable at a glance:

  • EMA 20 (yellow) — the fast line, roughly the last day's mood.
  • EMA 50 (orange) — the medium line, the week's drift.
  • EMA 200 (purple) — the slow line, the underlying tide.
The stack rule: LONGS need price above yellow, yellow ≥ orange, orange ≥ purple — fast above slow, all agreeing up. SHORTS need the mirror image. Lines tangled = no trade.

The lines are labelled on the chart (v2.2) so you never have to guess which is which.

Daily SMA 50 / 200 — the big-picture bias

Same idea, but on the daily chart (S = simple average), using yesterday's completed close so it can't repaint. Two checks make the daily bias:

BULL biasyesterday's close above the 200-day average AND 50-day above 200-day
BEAR biasboth conditions mirrored below
MIXEDanything else — no trades either direction

A signal needs BOTH the daily bias AND the H1 EMA stack agreeing with its direction. One without the other is a skip — this is the two-part trend filter.

ATR(14) — the volatility ruler

Average True Range: how much gold typically moves in one hour, averaged over the last 14 hours. If ATR is 11, a normal hour covers about $11. It's our ruler for "big" and "small" — the same rules work whether gold is at 2,000 or 5,000:

RuleUses ATR how
Breakout bufferclose must clear box + 0.05 × ATR
Box too smallrange under 0.30 × ATR = noise, skip
Box too bigrange over 3.00 × ATR = panic day, skip
Shock candlesingle candle over 3 × ATR = optional cooldown

The marker v2.2 — the checklist, drawn

The PROSPER marker isn't an indicator that "gives signals" — it's our tested checklist made visible: it builds the box, grades it (updates / spike / range), tracks both trend filters, and only prints BREAKOUT CLOSE when every gate passes. Then it draws the trade: red STOP zone, green TP zone, prices printed inside, zone ending at the 18-hour max hold.

Past signals stay on the chart, so scrolling back is a free lesson library. The Action row translates everything into one instruction: BUY / SELL / WAIT / TRADE TAKEN / DON'T TRADE.

The marker displays the rules — it doesn't replace the checklist. "The zone was green so I bought" is a rule breach even on a winning trade.

DXY — context on the side

The US dollar index. Gold is priced in dollars, so they often move opposite each other. The marker shows it as CTX (context) only: worth a line in the journal, never a reason to take or skip a trade. Testing never proved it improves the edge — so it stays decorative until it does.

The Hype Detector

Run every strategy video through these six questions. Tick what the video actually provides:

Sample size stated? (n = how many trades)
Results in R or expectancy — not just a win rate?
Out-of-sample or forward-tested, not just one lucky year?
Costs included? (spread/commission)
Rules precise enough that a computer could follow them?
Losing streaks / drawdowns shown honestly?
Score 5–6: worth a journal note. Score 3–4: entertainment with homework. Score 0–2: entertainment. For scale — our model answers all six: n=183, +0.479R OOS expectancy, PF 2.56, costed, coded, drawdowns known.

H4 London Breakout — Study/Demo Only

Promoted from the lab on 2026-07-04 (lab/exp_breakout_h4.py, pre-registered gates). It is not the funded model. It runs beside the H1 Asian model as a demo-forward candidate because it fixes the cadence problem: about 8 signals a month across XAU + NAS100 versus 1.9 on H1 alone.

Tested rules (frozen for study):

1. Mark the London morning range 08:00–12:00 UK (same box discipline as the Asian range).
2. Wait for an H4 candle CLOSE outside the range — closes land 13:00 / 17:00 / 21:00 UK. A wick is not a signal, same as H1.
3. Daily trend + EMA stack must agree with the break direction (same filter family as the funded model).
4. Stop: 1.0 × H4 ATR from entry. Target: 1.5R. Max hold 2 days. One new trade per market per day. 4h news block.
5. Skip shock candles (H4 range > 3 × ATR) and micro ranges (< 0.5 × ATR).

Backtest (8.5y, both markets, net of costs): 816 trades · OOS +0.33R · OOS PF 1.71 · 2026 holdout n=48 at +0.15R · FTMO sim ~99% pass, 0% breach at 0.25% risk.

Honest flags: XAU's own 2026 leg is negative (−0.16R, the holdout is carried by NAS100), and out-of-sample beat in-sample, which usually means recent regimes flatter it. Treat every live signal as evidence collection, not income.
Promotion to funded core requires: 3 months of demo-forward signals journalled, realized expectancy ≥ 50% of backtest, and the XAU leg proving itself in the current year. Until then it is a rule breach to fund this lane.

learning science

How This Course Makes It Stick

  • Dual coding: every rule is paired with a visual chart cue.
  • Worked examples: see the full trade plan before doing drills alone.
  • Retrieval practice: cards force recall before rereading.
  • Interleaving: drills mix clean range, wick traps, news blocks, work interruptions, and 2R decisions.
  • Spacing: card ratings now schedule the next review for today, tomorrow, 3 days, or 7 days.
  • Immediate feedback: practice reps show which rule passed or blocked the setup.
